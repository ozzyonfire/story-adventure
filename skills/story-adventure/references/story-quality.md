# Story Quality Guide

Apply this guide while creating outlines and each new scene.

## Core Criteria

1. Establish a clear player desire and stakes early.
2. Put meaningful obstacles between the player and that desire.
3. Escalate conflict over time; avoid flat repetition or busywork.
4. Offer meaningful choices with materially different outcomes.
5. Preserve continuity across facts, tone, timeline, and state.
6. Maintain scene pacing with a hook or pressure at scene end.

## Desire and Goal

- Give the protagonist one concrete central goal.
- State the desire in actionable terms: escape the manor, find the lost child, reach the tower before dawn.
- Avoid aimless atmosphere without a motivating objective.

## Obstacles and Friction

- Make the world push back in ways that force decisions.
- Prefer obstacles that create tradeoffs: a locked gate, a suspicious ally, dwindling lamp oil, a promise that conflicts with survival.
- Use friction to reveal character, not just to delay progress.

## Escalation

- Let early choices create later consequences.
- Increase complication, not just activity.
- Raise both practical stakes and emotional stakes where possible.
- Examples: a small lie becomes a betrayal; a missing key becomes a moral dilemma.

## Choice Quality Checks

For each set of options:

- Reject obvious choices, disguised duplicates, and purely cosmetic branches.
- Ensure each option changes risk, resource usage, alliances, or information state.
- Make the player trade something real: safety vs speed, honesty vs advantage, helping someone vs preserving resources, curiosity vs caution.
- Avoid duplicate options that only rephrase the same action.
- Keep option text action-oriented and specific.
- Keep option count consistent with skill rules (default: 3 options).

## Consistency and Surprise

- Keep the world internally coherent.
- Still allow outcomes to surprise the player in hindsight-consistent ways.
- Aim for: "That makes sense, and I did not see it coming."

## Specificity

- Prefer concrete, memorable details over generic mood statements.
- Replace vague description with particulars that imply story and tone.
- Example: use "a nursery where every rocking horse faces the window" instead of "a spooky room."

## Character Agency

- Give NPCs wants, fears, and leverage of their own.
- Do not use characters as passive lore dispensers.
- Let recurring characters complicate the player's decisions.

## Theme

- Give the story a light but legible thematic core.
- Common themes include greed, memory, cowardice, sacrifice, curiosity, and identity.
- Let the theme influence what choices cost and what endings mean.

## Continuity Checks

Before saving a turn:

- Confirm inventory/stats/flags/quests match `state.md` updates.
- Confirm new events do not contradict `world.md` constraints.
- Confirm protagonist motivation remains coherent with `outline.md`.
- Confirm chronology is consistent with previous scene logs.
- Confirm consequences from stealing, lying, hesitating, rescuing, or abandoning still matter later.

## Pacing and Tension

- Deliver a strong opening hook within the first turn.
- Use a compact structure: one central goal, a handful of recurring locations and characters, inventory with payoff, flags that alter later scenes, and multiple endings earned by choices.
- Alternate action, decision, and consequence beats.
- Use short-term consequences that feed longer-term stakes.
- Introduce new complications before resolving old ones too quickly.

## Ending Guidance

- Build toward endings foreshadowed by prior choices.
- Tie ending quality to accumulated flags, quests, and relationships.
- Ensure endings resolve central stakes, not only side events.

## Reward Attention

- Make the story reward attention.
- If the player notices clues, remembers names, keeps unusual items, or distrusts the right person, the world should respond.
- Use inventory and flags as dramatic memory, not decorative bookkeeping.
